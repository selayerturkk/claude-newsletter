Pricing Gets Real

Beginning June 15, Anthropic will separate programmatic Claude usage from standard chat subscription limits, introducing dedicated monthly credits billed at API-style rates. Pro users get $20 in credits, Max 5x users $100, and Max 20x users $200.

Broader Tech News

The Jobs Apocalypse That Wasn't

OpenAI CEO Sam Altman admitted he was "delighted to be wrong" about AI's impact on employment, saying he thought there would have been more impact on entry-level white-collar jobs by now than has actually happened. This represents a significant reversal from his earlier predictions that AI would eliminate most current jobs.

Altman realized that human interaction remains essential after experimenting with AI for his own communications, noting "we really do care about people" and "we really do care about our interactions with people". Meanwhile, a Yale Budget Lab study found no meaningful change in unemployment through March 2026 for workers in high AI exposure jobs.

Google's Aggressive Pricing Play

Google launched a $100/month AI Ultra plan for developers and technical leads while reducing their top-tier plan from $250 to $200. The company also moved from daily prompt limits to a "compute-used" model that factors in prompt complexity, features used, and chat length.

The price cuts suggest Google is feeling competitive pressure and would rather have more subscribers at lower margins than fewer subscribers watching from the sidelines. This puts pressure on OpenAI and Microsoft to respond with their own pricing moves.

Chip Wars Continue

Nvidia CEO Jensen Huang warned against splitting US and China AI ecosystems, acknowledging Nvidia has "largely conceded" China's AI chip market to Huawei after export controls. China previously accounted for more than 20% of Nvidia's data center revenue, but the company reported zero China revenue in its latest earnings.

Meanwhile, AMD kicked off production of its 6th Generation EPYC processors on TSMC's 2nm process, marking the first high-performance computing product at this node and signaling a direct challenge to Nvidia's AI compute dominance.

This week revealed a fascinating tension in AI leadership. While Anthropic is doubling down on security and enterprise capabilities, OpenAI is softening its rhetoric about job displacement. Google is aggressively cutting prices to gain market share. These aren't disconnected moves—they're symptoms of an industry transitioning from "move fast and break things" to "move fast and control things."

For aspiring product managers and CS students, the strategic lesson is clear: the companies winning long-term aren't just building better models—they're building better systems for deployment, governance, and trust.
